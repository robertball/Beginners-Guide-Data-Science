This is a fantastic recipe. I have had great success following it exactly and by making slight alterations. 
The recipe doesn't specify type of salt so I use pickling salt (dissolves in cold water easier, curing salt adds nitrites which are unnecessary if your dehydrator goes above 160 or if oven is used)
I have found that omitting the Liquid Smoke and adding 1tsp cayenne pepper and 1tsp curry is wonderful. 
We also make a "hot" version where we sub hot paprika, add 2tsp cayenne, 1tsp crushed reds and 1tsp curry

Warming the marinade allowed the brown sugar to dissolve and all the other ingredients to incorporate well. I used my oven to dry it, 180 degrees which is the lowest it goes, for about 4 hours.

This is an almost perfect recipe. I use a variation of this and it always gets two thumbs up from everyone who has tried it. I cook it in an oven at a low temperature and keep the door slightly ajar to prevent cooking the beef too quickly.

I left out the meat tenderizer as it is not needed, especially if you are using a nice top round beef. I recommend marinating for a maximum 30 minutes or you'll encounter an overload of salt from the soy and various powders.

If you marinate using this recipe over night like I did the first time, you're going to have one VERY salty beef jerky on your hands that will have you dry reaching.

Consider using smoked paprika and leaving out the liquid smoke and you get a slightly less jarring flavour that is more spicy. In place of the garlic powder I used freshly crushed garlic. Honestly, the difference is night and day if you use fresh garlic.

I also leave out the sugar as I don't like sweetened jerky. I prefer to let the meat do the flavour work and sugar to me just obscures the taste of the meat. If you want a more store bought taste, add in the sugar or just leave it out.

My improvements on the method made mine incredibly easy, not so messy to make, and delicious. First, I mix all the ingredients except the meat in a large ziplock bag and mix well. Then, I used flank steak, aka London Broil, that has been frozen and then thawed for an hour or so, so that I could slice it into 1/4-inch strips. I remove the fat on the edges as I discovered that it doesn't dry well or taste good after drying. Then I lay the bag(s) flat on a cookie sheet and store in the refrigerator for 2 to 3 days, or whenever I have time to dry them. No more than 3 days or else the meat tenderizer begins to turn the meat to mush.  I flip them every once in a while or when I think of it to ensure the marinade reaches every corner of the meat. When it's time to pound them flat and put in the dehydrator, I put two or three slices in a freezer ziplock bag (for strength) and pound them flat with a spiked mallet before laying them in the dehydrator.  They dry for 4-6 hours on high heat, and then I pack them and send them off to my son at school. At least I know he's eating SOMETHING tasty...

I try other recipes for fun but I keep going back to this because it's my favorite and everyone keeps requesting it.  No need to change a thing with the recipe but I've perfected my technique with it.  
Keep an eye on the sales flyers.  You can often find London broil, top round, and bottom round for $3/lb.  Don't buy just anything, even if it means leaving the store with nothing.  Look for cuts that have a large fat cap on one end with NO or nearly no marbling.  Meat should be deep red.  Light red means small veins of fat running through it.  Great for steaks and roast, not good for jerky.  Cut the fat cap off and slice your jerky.
Put all dry ingredients in a magic bullet.  Use Kosher salt and black peppercorns and any other whole spices if possible.  Much better flavor from your spices and seasonings that way.  Grind and mix.  Then add liquid ingredients and mix in the magic bullet.  Get a smaller bowl, pour the marinade in, dredge a strip and place it in another bowl large enough to fit your meat.  Really easy and ensures a more consistent flavor in each piece.  
If desired, sprinkle with course ground black pepper before dehydrating to get a peppered jerky.  Add as little or as much as you like but start with a little on your first batch so you don't ruin a good batch of jerky.
