I just finished making 42 half-pint jars of these peach preserves.  This was my first time to make preserves, and I found this recipe quite easy for beginners.  I loved that there is no peeling, no lemon squeezing, and no skimming to do.  I think the secret to success is to be sure to bring the fruit and sugar mixture to a gentle boil that continues even when stirred, before adding the pectin.  If you add the pectin too soon, your preserves may end up runny.  Also, remember that this is a preserve recipe.  Preserves don't set up like jelly.  They should be about the consistency of honey.  That's exactly what I got, and the flavor is wonderful!  Definitely a keeper!

Had some underripe peaches I didn't get to until they were soft.  Chopped up 10 cups, cooked for 20 min w/ some lemon juice & because they were dry, added juice of 2 oranges.  Added 3 1/2 c sugar to taste.  Cooked for 3-5 min.  Since the chopped peaches did not break down enough, I ground it up in the pan with one of those hand held blenders until I got a slightly mashed with little pieces of peaches texture.  It was thick enough that I didn't need any pectin which I think gives a better taste.  It was thick because the peaches were homegrown and were not fully ripe before picked.  If they had been juicy, I probably would have had to add pectin or an apple.  Roasted 2 poblano chilis and added peeled and finely chopped (w/o seeds) to 1/3 of recipe in addition 1/4-1/2 t pecorino for heat because the poblanos were not spicy enough for me.  This made a total of 2 1/2 - 12 oz jars of spicy preserves and 4 - 12 oz jars of regular preserves.    I often make preserves in this way w/o pectin.  Sometimes  I add an apple that contains a lot of pectin but not too much flavor to help thicken a juicy fruit preserve.  The less cooking you do with the sugar, the fresher and more bright the fruit tastes.  Yummy.

This recipe fills approximately 24-26 4oz Ball jam jars. Used this recipe to make 4oz jam jars as the guest favor at my wedding. Remove RIPE peach skin with the following process: Make a small X at the base of the peach with a paring knife. Dip in boiling water for about 30 seconds to a minute. Dunk hot peaches into a bowl of ice water. Slip off the skins, but be careful now, the naked peaches are slippery. Once all measured peaches are back in the pot along with the sugar, the chunks need to be smashed up according to the consistency of jam you prefer. This is easily done with a potato masher. 
I put a round white sticker on top of the jar lid after sterilizing the jars in a water bath (used my All American Canner, a marvelous canning accessory!) and wrote each guests' name on it. That doubled my guest favor as a place marker for the seating arrangement. My wedding guests loved the gift, got fantastic reviews. One guest used it as an ice cream topping. Others just spread it on toast. 
I made several batches and they varied in consistency, not sure if this was due to not keeping the jam in the hot pot long enough to thicken more or if the ripeness of the peaches (or rather, lack of) influenced the overall result. The only "complaint" I got was that (and mostly only the men said this), "It's too sweet." Otherwise, very good, simple recipe, will use again and again.

Made a preserve for the first time ever, based on this recipe and turned out fantastic. But I made some modifications. 1) Added 1 large lemon, in order to guarantee mixture will be acidic and prevent botulism. 2) Only used 3 cups - 3.5 cups of sugar as the recipe is too sweet. 3) Pureed half of the peaches, not 1/4 of them. 4) 2 oz of powdered pectin was IMPOSSIBLE to find in NYC. So I used 1/4 cup of "apple pectin tablets", crushed into a powder. I found this in a vitamin store. 5) Once the peaches are boiling, you can add the lemon, then sugar, then pectin directly into the pot and cook until 1 min after boiling. 6) Recipe wasn't clear on this but you should be stirring non-stop the mixture.  You must achieve boiling bubbles while stirring. Also, always add the hot mixture, to heated empty jars or they will crack.

Very simple, and tasty. If you end up with more, or less peaches than 6 cups, the ratio is:
1 cup cooked peaches: 3/4 sugar: 1 tablespoon pectin.

We used mostly under-ripe, hard peaches as instructed in the tips (about 2/3 of all of the peaches were still hard) and it turned out beautifully. Sweet, with just a touch of tart. Set, it reached a thick consistency halfway between cold honey and firm, cuttable jello.
The only changes we made were:
 1) blending the peach chunks since we prefer smooth (I am not sure  where some people in the comments are getting "leave the skin on," but we peeled them first)
2) adding Fruit Fresh Produce Protector (1 tsp per cup of cooked peaches, don't include sugar in that measurement, added at the same time as the pectin) It helps prevent the peach mixture from going brown and the extra acid helps the jam set.
Tips:
-The cooking times are from the onset of a boil (meaning the bubbles don't stop or go away when you stir). Don't start the timer at the first sight of a bubble or things will be underdone and not set. This is especially important if you're making a double batch.
-Stir this a lot to prevent the bottom scorching, but stop and do not stir after the sugar+peaches begins to boil.
-Do not increase the temperature to reduce the cook time. It's tempting with large, slow to heat, batches, but don't. You'll get burnt, caramel-y peach jam.

The only reason for 4 stars instead of 5 is that I think the amount of peaches should be more precise.  Peaches come in lots of sizes, so "12" is a bit vague.
