For those who found it grainy... powdered sugar won't make it grainy only real sugar - make sure you use powdered sugar.  For those who found it too runny: the egg whites need to be beaten until frothy first, then add powdered sugar and beat until you get the consistency you want, THEN add half the lemon juice gradually while you continue beating.  If it's still too thick add additional lemon juice.  I use these ingredients all the time to make royal icing (which technically isn't used to "ice" things but to make decorations) and have never had a problem with it being too runny or soft.  The consistency depends entirely on how well you beat the egg whites and powdered sugar - longer = stiffer - just like making meringue.  If you add the juice too soon it may not stiffen up properly.   If you want a good recipe for "coating" cookies use this one: 1 c powdered sugar, 2 Tblsp honey or white cane syrup, 1 Tblsp milk, 1/4 tsp almond flavoring - mix sugar and honey then add milk to get the right consistency (may add more if you want it thinner but don't make too thin - if you make it too thin add more sugar -  but try to keep the ratios balanced) then add flavoring.  It coats well and air dries quick without refrigerating.  I brush it onto my sugar cookies as a base coat before decorating with piped icing.

Adjust the sugar and lemon juice (or water) for your desired consistency. To 'flood' a cookie and get a smooth finish you would want the icing a little loose and I would probably start with 3 cups of sugar. If you beat our egg whites enough it will stiffen up quickly (any yolk will prevent egg whites from stiffening). Put cookies in the fridge or freezer to stiffen quicker if you plan on piping on decorations after the initial base of frosting. This recipe does need to be thicker, or of piping consistency, to pipe on more intricate decorations. This means almost as thick as creamy peanut butter (sorry that's the best thing I can think of to compare to). To achieve this consistency simply add more powder sugar.

In my opinion, the negative comments about this icing are made by people who don't really know what Royal Icing is supposed to be like.  This is a good icing and it turned out exactly as I expected it to. If you want it a little stiffer, add more powdered sugar. If you want it a little softer, add less. It's supposed to be sweet and it's supposed to be pure white. The egg whites ensure the color as well as the texture and consistency. In the UK (where I'm from), royal icing is commonly used on Christmas cakes, Wedding cakes, Christening cakes - any kind of rich fruit cake. It is not commonly used on cookies.  This is a very versatile icing and can be left a little rough or be smoothed into a perfectly flat and level surface, depending on what you require. It also hardens at it dries so provides a good base for adding tiers. It is supposed to harden as it dries, it is the nature of the icing. I believe this can be avoided somewhat if you add glycerine but I have never done that and don't know how much you would use. Overall, it is a very good recipe which I have used many, many times with no issues.
